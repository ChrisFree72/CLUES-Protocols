⚠️ Glassware Restriction Only furnaced Pyrex test tubes and serological pipets are to be used for sample transfer, storage, and preparation. No other glassware should be used.
🔥

Furnacing Equipment

  1. Count the number of test tubes required based on sample volume or mass. See the Quick Reference table for per-sample tube requirements.
  2. Place test tubes and serological pipets in the furnace for decontamination.
  3. Run the furnace program to reach 400°C, which removes manufacturing additives and contaminants from glassware.
  4. Allow the furnace to cool to 25°C before opening. Allow an additional ~30 minutes for glassware to cool fully before handling.
  5. Furnaced glassware is now ready for use in sample transfer, storage, and preparation.

Preparing Sample Aliquots

Blood & Urine — Lab Aliquoting (Storage)

📌 Storage aliquots: Blood and Urine These samples are aliquoted in the lab into storage tubes using furnaced glassware. Fill to no more than ¼ total tube volume (max 5mL per 20mL tube) to allow for freeze expansion. Urine should be transferred to storage tubes and refrigerated as quickly as possible after collection.

Blood, Urine, Saliva & Seminal Fluid — Digestion Aliquots (1mL per tube)

📌 Digestion aliquots: Blood, Urine, Saliva, and Seminal Fluid Digestion aliquots are 1mL into a 20mL tube. The sample will be digested directly in this same tube — it serves as both the storage and digestion vessel. Blood and urine digestion aliquots are prepared in the lab. Saliva and seminal fluid digestion aliquots must be prepared by the collector before freezing (see below). For urine, bring to room temperature and mix thoroughly after thawing to resolubilize any crystals before digestion begins.
  1. Using a furnaced serological pipet, transfer exactly 1mL of sample slowly along the inner tube wall into a furnaced 20mL test tube.
  2. Cover with aluminum foil and cap with PTFE cap.
  3. Label clearly and proceed to pre-cooling and freezing per the Freezing section.

Saliva & Seminal Fluid — Collector Pre-Aliquoting

📌 Applies to: Saliva and Seminal Fluid These samples must be aliquoted by the collector into pre-furnished, pre-labeled tubes before freezing. Post-freeze pipetting of these fluids is unreliable due to viscosity changes.
  • Saliva: Pipet 1mL directly into the digestion tube and seal. Refrigerate promptly.
  • Seminal fluid: Allow the sample to liquefy at room temperature for 20–60 minutes before aliquoting. Freezing before liquefaction produces a gelled, heterogeneous mass that is not workable. Once liquefied, pipet 1mL into the digestion tube, seal, and refrigerate.

Stool — Slurry Preparation & Aliquoting

📌 Applies to: Stool Stool is inherently heterogeneous. A slurry must be prepared before aliquoting to ensure representative sub-samples. Ultrapure filtered water must be used for slurry preparation.
  1. Weigh the total stool sample mass using an analytical balance.
  2. Add ultrapure filtered water at a ratio of 1mL water per 1g stool (1:1 w/v) to the sample container.
  3. Mix thoroughly until the slurry is as homogeneous as possible.
  4. Aliquot up to 5mL of slurry per 20mL tube (¼ volume rule applies — slurry is largely aqueous and will expand upon freezing).
  5. Cover with aluminum foil and cap with PTFE cap. Refrigerate immediately.
💧 Filtered Water Note Ultrapure filtered water for stool slurry preparation may be provided by our lab. Contact us if you require pre-filtered water to be supplied alongside your furnaced equipment.
⚠️ Gas Pressure Warning Microbial activity in stool samples can generate gas even at low temperatures. Always open stool containers slowly and work in a fume hood.
❄️

Freezing All Samples

📌 This procedure applies to all sample types: blood, urine, saliva, seminal fluid, stool, and tissue.
  1. Ensure all tubes are sealed with aluminum foil under the PTFE cap before proceeding.
  2. Place sealed sample tubes upright in an appropriate rack for the tube size.
  3. Load the rack into a refrigerator or cold room for a minimum of 2 hours to pre-cool the samples gradually.
  4. Transfer the pre-cooled rack to the −20°C freezer.
⚠️ Never Place Samples Directly in the −20°C Freezer All samples must be pre-cooled in the refrigerator before transfer to −20°C. Placing biological samples directly into −20°C significantly increases the risk of glass cracking due to rapid thermal shock and freeze expansion.
📋

Fluid-Specific Storage Considerations

Container Risk

Highest cracking risk — highly aqueous, expands significantly upon freezing. Strict ¼ volume fill rule for storage tubes. Digestion aliquots are 1mL in a 20mL tube so headspace is not a concern for those.

Pre-freeze Handling

Refrigerator pre-cooling for minimum 2 hours before −20°C. Lab-aliquoted using furnaced glassware.

Digestion Notes

Sample is digested directly in the storage tube. Ensure sample is well mixed after thawing before beginning KOH digestion.

Special Notes

Transfer slowly along tube wall to minimize pipet adhesion. Foil under cap on all tubes.

Container Risk

Similar to blood — largely aqueous with significant freeze expansion. Apply ¼ volume fill rule (max 5mL per 20mL tube) for all storage aliquots. Digestion aliquots (1mL in 20mL) pose no expansion concern.

Pre-freeze Handling

Refrigerate immediately after collection. Urine supports rapid bacterial growth at room temperature. Minimize delay between collection and refrigeration.

Digestion Notes

Crystals may form upon freezing. Bring to room temperature and mix thoroughly before sub-sampling or beginning KOH digestion to resolubilize crystals and ensure a representative aliquot.

Special Notes

Easiest fluid to work with overall. Aqueous nature is highly compatible with KOH digestion. Both storage and digestion aliquots are lab-prepared.

Container Risk

Lower cracking risk than blood or urine — mucin content slightly moderates expansion. Digestion aliquots (1mL in 20mL tube) pose no expansion concern.

Pre-freeze Handling

Must be aliquoted by collector (1mL per digestion tube) before freezing. Viscosity increases dramatically after freeze-thaw, making post-freeze pipetting unreliable. Refrigerate promptly after collection.

Digestion Notes

Mucin precipitation after thaw can cause heterogeneity. Vortex thoroughly before KOH digestion. Sample is digested in the same tube it was collected into.

Special Notes

Collector must be provided with pre-labeled, furnaced, capped tubes. Instructions must be communicated clearly in advance.

Container Risk

Lower cracking risk than aqueous samples due to high protein and lipid content dampening expansion. Digestion aliquots (1mL in 20mL) pose no expansion concern.

Pre-freeze Handling

Critical: Must liquefy at room temperature 20–60 min before aliquoting. Freezing before liquefaction produces a gelled, non-homogeneous mass that is not workable.

Digestion Notes

Higher residual viscosity than other fluids. Mix thoroughly before KOH digestion. Sample is digested in the same tube it was collected into.

Special Notes

Most practically challenging fluid. Collector instructions regarding the liquefaction wait time must be explicit and communicated before collection.

Container Risk

Freeze-expansion risk reduced by heterogeneous solid matrix. Primary container concern is gas pressure from microbial activity. Open containers slowly in a fume hood.

Pre-freeze Handling

Prepare 1:1 w/v slurry with ultrapure filtered water before aliquoting. Refrigerate as quickly as possible after collection. Mix until as homogeneous as possible before aliquoting.

Digestion Notes

Even as a slurry, stool remains the most heterogeneous sample type. Mix thoroughly after thawing before sub-sampling for digestion. Open thawed tubes slowly in a fume hood.

Special Notes

Most difficult sample type for representative sub-sampling. The 1:1 slurry preparation is the most effective mitigation strategy available at this step. Filtered water may be supplied by our lab.

Tissue Samples — Breast & Placenta

📌 Applies to: Breast Tissue and Placental Tissue Tissue samples are stored as solid pieces — no homogenization or slurry preparation is performed before freezing. Each aliquot consists of 1–5g of tissue stored in a single furnaced 20mL tube. The same furnacing, sealing, pre-cooling, and freezing procedures apply.

Tissue Aliquoting

  1. Using furnaced forceps and scissors, section the tissue into individual aliquots of 1–5g each. Verify mass using an analytical balance.
  2. Place each tissue aliquot into a separate furnaced 20mL test tube. A single piece per tube is preferred to avoid compression or overlapping.
  3. No liquid or buffer is added. The tube should contain only the tissue aliquot.
  4. Cover the tube opening with a square of aluminum foil, then screw the PTFE cap on over the foil to seal.
  5. Label each tube clearly with sample ID, tissue type, and aliquot mass.

Freezing Tissue

📌 Same freezing procedure as all other sample types. Place sealed tubes upright in a rack, pre-cool in the refrigerator for a minimum of 2 hours, then transfer to the −20°C freezer. Do not place directly in the −20°C freezer.

Breast Tissue

Fatty tissue with variable density. Aim for uniform aliquot sizes within a sample set where possible. Tissue does not expand upon freezing — container cracking is not a concern. The primary consideration is consistent aliquot mass for downstream digestion reproducibility.

Placental Tissue

More vascular and heterogeneous than breast tissue. Where possible, sample from a consistent region of the placenta across subjects to reduce inter-sample variability. Excess blood should be blotted (not rinsed) from the tissue surface before weighing and aliquoting.
